When does a combined wave repeat?

Build sin(kx + φ) + cos(mx) and see the fundamental period — not memorize LCM. Scroll: each section adds one idea. Tweak, predict, watch the brackets.

What you'll be able to do after ~10 minutes
  • Point at the green bracket and say what period it measures.
  • Predict: doubling k halves T; sliding φ leaves T unchanged.
  • Explain why sin(2x) + cos(3x) repeats at 2π (LCM of π and 2π/3).

1 · Period

Start with sin(x). Treat x as time in seconds (ω = 1 rad/s) — one peak-to-peak wiggle is the period T.

2 · Speed it up inside

Scaling the inside of sine compresses the wave — sin(kx) fits k cycles where one used to fit. Period becomes 2π/k.

Only where zeros land changes when you scale k. The count per period is tied to frequency — you're not creating new zeros out of nowhere.

3 · Phase is not period

Adding φ shifts the wave sideways. The shape still repeats at the same rate — T stays the same (watch the green label pulse unchanged).

▸ why phase doesn't change period

Translation along x is not a new repetition rate. f(x+φ) slides the graph; f(kx) compresses it.

4 · Two waves, one pattern

Add cos(mx). The combined wave repeats only when both parts have finished whole cycles — that's LCM of their periods.

○Turn on cos(mx) — tap m = 3 — and watch the small brackets on each part.

▸ where LCM comes from

At x = T, both sin and cos must return to the same phase they had at x = 0. That needs an integer number of cycles for each — first x where both align is LCM(T₁, T₂).

▸ GCD and beat frequency

Between LCM alignments, the two rates "beat" against each other. Beat spacing relates to GCD(k, m) — when one rate divides the other, syncs are extra tight.
