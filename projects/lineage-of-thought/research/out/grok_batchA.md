Pythagoras

Pythagoras of Samos founded a religious-philosophical brotherhood in Croton (Magna Graecia) around 530 BCE. He taught that the cosmos is ordered by mathematical ratios and harmony, that the soul is immortal and undergoes reincarnation (metempsychosis), and that ethical purification leads to liberation. His school combined mathematics, music theory, astronomy, and ascetic discipline; though his own writings are lost, his ideas profoundly shaped Plato and later Western thought.

Heraclitus

Heraclitus of Ephesus, active around 500 BCE, is known for his cryptic fragments emphasizing constant change ("you cannot step twice into the same river") and the unity of opposites. He posited fire as the fundamental element and logos as the hidden rational structure ordering the cosmos through tension and strife. His process philosophy influenced Plato's distinction between sensible flux and stable Forms, as well as later Stoic and Hegelian thought.

Socrates

Socrates of Athens (c. 470–399 BCE) left no writings but transformed philosophy into ethical inquiry through the elenchus (Socratic method) of questioning assumptions. He prioritized the soul's virtue and knowledge over wealth or power, famously accepting death rather than compromising his principles. His life and method inspired Plato, Xenophon, the Cynics, and Stoics, founding Western moral philosophy.

Epicurus founded "the Garden" school in Athens (306 BCE), a community open to women and slaves. Drawing on atomism (Democritus) and hedonism, he taught that pleasure is the absence of bodily pain and mental disturbance; simple living, friendship, and understanding nature free one from fear of death and divine punishment. Zeno of Citium

Zeno of Citium, shipwrecked in Athens around 312 BCE, studied under Cynic Crates and founded Stoicism in the Stoa Poikile. He synthesized Socratic ethics, Cynic asceticism, and Heraclitean physics into a system where virtue alone ensures happiness, the cosmos is rationally ordered by divine logos (fire), and one must accept fate with equanimity. His school dominated Hellenistic and Roman philosophy.

Plotinus, born in Roman Egypt, studied in Alexandria under Ammonius Saccas before teaching in Rome from ~245 CE. He systematized Platonism into Neoplatonism: a hierarchical emanation from the transcendent One, through Intellect (nous) and World-Soul, to matter. Laozi

Laozi ("Old Master") is a legendary figure traditionally credited with authoring the Tao Te Ching in the 6th century BCE (or compiled 4th–3rd c.). The text advocates returning to the Dao — the spontaneous, nameless source of the universe — through wu wei (effortless action in accord with nature), humility, and reversal of conventional values. It birthed philosophical and religious Daoism.

Confucius

Confucius (Kongzi) of Lu, active during the Spring and Autumn period, sought to restore Zhou ritual order through moral education. He taught that self-cultivation in ren (humaneness), li (ritual), and yi (righteousness) produces the junzi who governs by virtue, not force. The Analects record his conversations emphasizing filial piety, learning, and benevolent rule. His thought became state orthodoxy and shaped East Asian civilization.

Mozi

Mozi (Mo Di) founded Mohism, a utilitarian and egalitarian school rivaling Confucianism. He advocated jian ai (impartial, universal love), meritocracy ("honor the worthy"), frugality, and defensive warfare only. Rejecting Confucian ritual hierarchy and graded affection, he grounded ethics in Heaven's impartial will and practical benefit (gongli). His followers organized into disciplined paramilitary communities.

Mencius

Mencius (Mengzi), "Second Sage" of Confucianism, defended and developed Confucius's teachings against rival schools. He argued human nature (xing) is good, possessing innate moral sprouts (commiseration, shame, deference, approval/disapproval) that must be nurtured. Rulers should govern benevolently; people have the right to revolt against tyranny. His optimistic anthropology and political theory shaped Neo-Confucianism.

Xunzi

Xunzi (Xun Kuang), teacher of Han Feizi and Li Si, offered a realist Confucian alternative to Mencius. He argued human nature (xing) tends toward disorder and self-interest; morality and social harmony are artificial achievements produced by ritual, education, and institutional design modeled on ancient sage-kings. His emphasis on li (ritual) as civilizing force and empirical observation influenced Legalism and later ritual studies.

Han Feizi

Han Feizi, aristocrat of Han and student of Xunzi, synthesized Legalist thought into a coherent political philosophy. He argued that in a large, warring state, Confucian virtue and Mohist universal love are ineffective; rulers must rely on objective laws, bureaucratic techniques, and the "two handles" of reward and punishment. His ideas helped Qin unify China but led to his own imprisonment and death.
