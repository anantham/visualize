# Zhuangzi

Zhuangzi (Zhuang Zhou) radicalized Daoist thought with humor, paradox, and vivid parables (butterfly dream, useless tree, butcher Ding). He advocated wu wei as effortless skill, acceptance of constant change (things "transform"), and freedom from artificial categories and social roles. Skeptical of language and conventional knowledge, he celebrated spontaneous harmony with the Dao over Confucian ritual or Mohist utility.

## Influenced by
- [[Laozi]] — develops Dao, wu wei, and naturalness into more skeptical, playful philosophy  _(✓2)_
- [[Hui Shi]] — paradox, disputation, and relativizing perspectives

## Develops
- [[Laozi]] — expands Daoist noncoercion into perspectival freedom and spiritual wandering

## Disagreed with
- [[Confucius]] :: Is ritual central to order? — rigid categories and rituals hinder spontaneous Dao-following; "usefulness of the useless"  _(✓2)_
- [[Mozi]] :: Should society be engineered by standards? — fixed standards miss the fluidity of Dao
- [[Hui Shi]] :: Can language state ultimate truth? — clever distinctions cannot capture the Dao

## Cruxes
- [[Can language state ultimate truth?]] — no; language is perspectival and limited
- [[Is ritual central to order?]] — no; spontaneous accord exceeds ritual
- [[Is the self real?]] — the fixed self is unstable and transformable
- [[What is the fundamental principle of reality?]] — Dao as constant transformation; freedom in wandering beyond distinctions

## Idea travel
- 300 — China: xuanxue thinkers read Zhuangzi with Laozi and the Yijing
- 300 — China (Warring States): Zhuangzi text circulates; influences later Daoist thought
- 320 — Song/Chu region: Zhuangzi stories circulate among Warring States literati
- 700 — Tang China: Zhuangzi becomes central to elite poetry, painting, and Daoist thought
- 1200 — East Asia: Chan/Zen traditions absorb Zhuangzian language of spontaneity
