# Han Feizi

Han Feizi, aristocrat of Han and student of Xunzi, synthesized Legalist thought into a coherent political philosophy. He argued that in a large, warring state, Confucian virtue and Mohist universal love are ineffective; rulers must rely on objective laws, bureaucratic techniques, and the "two handles" of reward and punishment. His ideas helped Qin unify China but led to his own imprisonment and death.

## Influenced by

- [[Xunzi]] — turns the need for artificial order into a hard theory of state power

## Disagreed with
- [[Confucius]] :: Is ritual central to order? — no; self-interested humans require clear laws, rewards, and punishments; virtue politics is naive  _(✓2)_
- [[Mencius]] :: Can virtue govern politics? — benevolence cannot reliably govern ministers and subjects
- [[Mozi]] :: Should care be impartial? — public order requires law and power, not moralized care

## Cruxes
- [[Can virtue govern politics?]] — no; institutions must assume self-interest
- [[Should society be engineered by standards?]] — yes; law and administrative technique must structure conduct
- [[Is ritual central to order?]] — no; law is more reliable than ritual
- [[What is the best way to govern?]] — impersonal laws (fa), techniques (shu), and positional power (shi) over moral cultivation
